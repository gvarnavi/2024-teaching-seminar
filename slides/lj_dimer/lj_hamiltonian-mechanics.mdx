## Hamiltonian Mechanics

:::note[Hamilton's Equations Reminder]
Recall that for time-independent potentials, we can define the **Lagrangian** as:
$$
\mathcal{L}\left(\left\{q \right\},\left\{\dot{q}\right\}\right) = \mathcal{T} - \mathcal{V},
$$
where $\mathcal{T}$ and $\mathcal{V}$ are the total kinetic and potential energies, and $q_i$ is the coordinate of particle $i$.  
This allows us to define a **canonical momentum** conjugate:
$$
p_i \equiv \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot{q}_i},
$$
and use it to express the **Hamiltonian** as:
$$
\mathcal{H}\left(\left\{ q \right\}, \left\{ p \right\} \right) = \sum_i p_i \dot{q}_i - \mathcal{L}\left(\left\{ q \right\}, \left\{ \dot{q} \right\} \right) = \mathcal{T} + \mathcal{V}.
$$
Hamilton's canonical equations are then given by:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\partial \mathcal{H}}{\partial p_i} &= \dot{q}_i \\
\frac{\partial \mathcal{H}}{\partial q_i} &= - \dot{p}_i
\end{aligned}
$$

:::
